NOTCH2 cell size morphology cytoskeleton

Notch2 signaling has been linked to the regulation of cell size and morphology in various cell types, including neural stem cells (NSCs), glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) cells, and human hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) cells. Research has shown that Notch2 signaling can promote tumorigenic features and astroglial lineage entry in NSCs

(
Tchorz et al. 2012
)
, leading to an increase in cell size and a more immature morphology. Similarly, in GBM cells, Notch2 signaling has been shown to increase cell size and prevent apoptosis, leading to a more aggressive phenotype
(
Tchorz et al. 2012
)
.

In HCC cells, Notch2 signaling has been associated with immature morphology and aggressiveness. The study found that HCC cells with high Notch2 expression had a more immature morphology and were more frequently positive for hepatoblast markers, such as AFP and CK19

(
Hayashi et al. 2015
)
. Furthermore, the study found that Notch2 expression was associated with the loss of E-cadherin expression, a marker protein for differentiated epithelium.

The cytoskeleton is a complex network of filaments that provides structural support and shape to cells. Notch2 signaling has been shown to regulate the cytoskeleton in various cell types, including HCC cells. The study found that Notch2 signaling regulates the expression of actin and tubulin, two key components of the cytoskeleton

(
Hayashi et al. 2015
)
. The regulation of the cytoskeleton by Notch2 signaling may contribute to the changes in cell size and morphology observed in Notch2-positive HCC cells.

Overall, the evidence suggests that Notch2 signaling plays a crucial role in regulating cell size and morphology in various cell types, including NSCs, GBM cells, and HCC cells. The regulation of the cytoskeleton by Notch2 signaling may be a key mechanism underlying these changes

(
Hayashi et al. 2015
)
. Further research is needed to fully understand the mechanisms by which Notch2 signaling regulates cell size and morphology.
